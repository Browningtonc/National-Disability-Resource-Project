About us

This project was started by Colton Brown. Colton grew up with a little sister with Down syndrome, and his family had a difficult time finding clear and trustworthy information about healthcare, services, and support options.

Today, he is also the father of a son with cerebral palsy and epilepsy. Like many families, he is regularly navigating appointments, specialists, public programs, and the day-to-day challenge of finding the right care. Through that process, he has seen how hard it can be to find information that is practical, easy to understand, and organized in one place.

This website is a passion project built to help more people understand their options, learn how care systems work in their state, and explore whether another state may offer better access to the services their family needs. The goal is simple. Make information easier to find, easier to compare, and easier to use.

This website is offered as a public information resource only. It is not medical advice, legal advice, financial advice, insurance advice, or professional case guidance. Every family, diagnosis, and state system is different, so users should confirm important information with their own doctors, care teams, state agencies, attorneys, benefits counselors, or other qualified professionals before making decisions.

Early intervention ages 0 to 3

A major federal program for very young children is the early intervention program under IDEA Part C. This program operates through statewide systems in all 50 states and helps identify infants and toddlers with developmental delays or diagnosed conditions that may lead to developmental delays.

The program is designed to support development as early as possible, connect families with evaluations and services, and help parents and caregivers support their child in everyday life. Early support can matter because the first years of life are a key period for growth and development.

State programs may use different names and have different referral processes, but the federal program exists nationwide. Families who have concerns about a child’s development can usually ask for an evaluation through their state’s early intervention system.

Who it is for

IDEA Part C generally serves infants and toddlers from birth through age 2 who have developmental delays or have diagnosed physical or mental conditions with a high probability of resulting in developmental delay.

What it can include

Early intervention may address physical development, communication, cognitive development, social and emotional development, and adaptive skills. Services are individualized based on the child and family.

How services are planned

Services are generally organized through an Individualized Family Service Plan, often called an IFSP. Federal guidance says services should be provided in natural environments to the maximum extent appropriate.

Why early referral matters

Families often do not hear about early intervention soon enough. If there are concerns about development, an early referral can help families get an evaluation, understand next steps, and connect with support during an important stage of development.

Important age note

IDEA Part C generally covers birth through age 2. Transition planning for services after age 3 is an important part of the process, and the next step is often preschool special education or another state program.

Katie Beckett style waivers and pathways

A Katie Beckett style waiver or pathway is a Medicaid route that can help some children with significant disabilities or complex medical needs qualify for Medicaid at home even when family income would otherwise be too high for standard Medicaid. The exact structure varies by state.

In simple terms, these pathways are meant to keep children from having to remain in hospitals or institutions just to access coverage. They generally focus on the child’s disability-related needs, level of care, and cost-effectiveness rules rather than using the parents’ income in the usual way.

What families usually look for

Families often want to know whether their state has a Katie Beckett style option, what age range it covers, whether parent income is disregarded, whether there are premiums or fees, and whether the pathway provides full Medicaid or a narrower set of supports.

Important reminder

Program names, eligibility rules, and administrative structure differ by state. Some states call it Katie Beckett, some use TEFRA language, and others operate a similar pathway under a different name.

What this site is using as the baseline

This site uses each state’s most relevant child-serving Medicaid pathway as the starting point. Sometimes that is Katie Beckett. Sometimes it is a waiver or another child-focused Medicaid route.

National nonprofit resources

This section highlights widely used national nonprofit and family-support resources that can help with advocacy, education, caregiver support, disability rights, service navigation, and long-term planning. These are strong starting points when someone needs help beyond a state Medicaid page.

The Arc

The Arc is one of the best-known disability advocacy organizations in the country, especially for people with intellectual and developmental disabilities and their families. It is helpful for policy updates, future planning tools, Medicaid advocacy, and connecting with local chapters.

Easterseals

Easterseals has a large national network that helps children and adults with disabilities, caregivers, and older adults. It is useful when people need practical services, caregiver support, local programs, benefits guidance, and community-based help.

Family Voices

Family Voices is a family-led organization focused on children and youth with special health care needs and disabilities. It helps families understand health systems, advocate for better care, and connect with parent-led support and policy work.

Parent to Parent USA

Parent to Parent USA connects families with other parents who have lived through similar disability and care experiences. It is especially helpful for emotional support, practical advice, and reducing the isolation that can come with raising a child with significant needs.

National Disability Rights Network

NDRN connects people to the protection and advocacy system in every state and territory. It is one of the most useful places to start when someone needs legal advocacy, rights protection, help with abuse or neglect issues, or disability civil rights support.

DREDF

The Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund focuses on civil rights, policy, and legal advocacy. It is especially helpful for understanding disability rights law, education rights, access issues, and broader policy changes that affect disabled people and families.

Parent Center Hub

Parent Center Hub is one of the most useful education-focused resources for families of children with disabilities. It helps parents understand IEPs, special education rights, early intervention, transition planning, and how to find their local Parent Training and Information Center.

United Cerebral Palsy

United Cerebral Palsy offers information, referrals, and local affiliate services for people with cerebral palsy and many other disabilities. It can be helpful for families looking for community programs, support services, and disability-specific guidance that still has broad practical value.

Autism Society

The Autism Society is a widely used support and referral resource for autistic people and their families. It helps people find information, local resources, education materials, and support connections through both national and state-level networks.

Special Needs Alliance

Special Needs Alliance is especially useful for families who need help with legal planning, special needs trusts, benefits protection, and long-term financial planning. It is a strong next step when a family is trying to think beyond immediate care and plan for the future.

Where to start

This page is designed for families, caregivers, and disabled people who are trying to find a clear first step. It is common to feel unsure where to begin, especially when diagnoses, school needs, equipment requests, insurance issues, and Medicaid questions are all happening at once. The goal of this page is to help families identify a practical starting point and the kind of information that usually matters first.

My child was just diagnosed

A diagnosis often creates new questions about medical care, therapy, insurance, school services, early intervention, and long-term planning. A practical place to begin is by gathering the child’s diagnosis records, specialist notes, current prescriptions, and therapy recommendations, then identifying whether the next immediate need is medical support, school support, or public-benefits support.

Families may also want to begin learning whether the child may qualify for Medicaid, early intervention, special education services, waiver programs, or other state disability supports. Rules can vary by state, program, and eligibility category, so current program details should be verified directly with the appropriate agency or provider.

Medicaid denied equipment

When equipment is denied, one of the most important first steps is to get the denial reason in writing and keep a copy of the full notice. Families may also want to gather any prescription, therapy evaluation, prior authorization request, and letter of medical necessity connected to the equipment request.

It is often helpful to determine whether the issue is medical necessity, missing documentation, a coverage limitation, or a problem with the way the request was submitted. Families may then want to review appeal options, look for assistive technology programs, or explore equipment funding resources if a coverage gap remains.

We need in-home care

When the need for in-home care becomes urgent, families may want to begin by identifying whether the main need is nursing, personal care, daily supervision, behavioral support, or help with activities of daily living. That distinction often affects which Medicaid pathway, waiver, or service model may be relevant.

Families may also want to gather recent medical records, discharge paperwork, therapy notes, and documentation showing why care is needed in the home. Because in-home care rules vary by state, program, and level of care, current details should be verified directly with the state Medicaid agency, managed care plan, or program involved.

We need respite

Respite is often one of the first supports families look for, but it can be offered through different systems. In some cases it may be part of a waiver or long-term-support program. In other cases it may come from nonprofit programs, county disability services, or diagnosis-specific organizations.

A practical place to begin is by identifying whether the child or adult is already enrolled in a waiver, long-term-support program, or state disability service system. If not, families may want to ask whether respite is available through current Medicaid services, county programs, or community organizations while longer-term options are being explored.

We are trying to understand waiver options

Waiver systems can be difficult to understand because they are often separate from basic Medicaid eligibility. A helpful first step is to identify whether the person already qualifies for Medicaid, whether the state has a child-specific or adult-specific pathway that fits the diagnosis or level of need, and whether the program has an interest list or waitlist.

Families may find it helpful to begin with three questions: what is the exact name of the program, who it is for, and whether it is an eligibility pathway, a service pathway, or both. That can make it much easier to compare options and avoid confusion between standard Medicaid, waivers, and special child programs.

We are trying to find out whether a parent can be paid

Paid parent and family caregiving rules vary widely. A practical place to begin is by identifying the exact service or program involved, because Medicaid eligibility alone does not answer whether a parent or family member can be paid to provide care.

Families may want to ask whether the service is provided through a waiver, a state plan benefit, or a self-directed model, and whether legally responsible relatives are excluded. Current rules should be verified directly with the state, managed care plan, or waiver administrator responsible for the program.

We are considering moving to another state for better care

Families who are considering a move often need more than one answer. It is usually helpful to compare child or adult Medicaid pathways, waiver options, parent-caregiver rules, known waitlist concerns, equipment access, specialist access, and transition-to-adulthood systems before making decisions.

Because disability systems are shaped by both state rules and local implementation, families may also want to look at provider availability, children’s hospitals, school supports, county systems, and local disability networks. Public materials do not always describe these local differences clearly, so direct calls and local parent input can be important.

We do not know which records to gather first

When everything feels urgent, it can help to begin with the documents that are most often requested across systems. These usually include diagnosis records, recent evaluations, prescriptions, therapy notes, insurance notices, Medicaid notices, denial letters, and contact information for the professionals involved in care.

Many families also find it useful to keep a simple care notebook or digital folder with call notes, dates, reference numbers, and copies of important letters. That kind of organization can make later applications, appeals, and waiver conversations much easier.

Important reminder

This page is a starting guide, not a substitute for state-specific advice or program-specific instructions. Rules can vary by state, program, insurer, managed care plan, age, diagnosis, and level of care.

Families should verify current program details directly with the state, insurer, school system, provider, or organization involved before relying on any specific next step.

The best first call can vary depending on the program involved, the state, the school district, the insurer, and the person’s diagnosis or level of need. Even so, there are common starting points that can help families move more quickly toward the right support.

Medicaid denied equipment

Families may want to begin with the prescribing provider, therapist, equipment vendor, or the Medicaid plan or agency that issued the denial. A strong first step is often to ask for the denial reason in writing, confirm whether prior authorization was submitted, and identify what supporting records may be missing.

We need in-home care

Families may want to begin with the state Medicaid agency, managed care plan, hospital discharge planner, primary care office, or waiver intake program, depending on how urgent the need is. The best first call can vary depending on whether the need is nursing, personal care, supervision, or broader long-term support.

We need respite

Families may want to begin with any current waiver, disability-service, or Medicaid support program already involved, and then ask whether respite is part of the current system. If no formal service system is in place yet, state disability agencies, nonprofit family-support organizations, and diagnosis-specific organizations may be useful starting points.

We are trying to get on a waiver

Families may want to begin with the state Medicaid agency, developmental-disability agency, waiver intake office, or long-term-support program responsible for the exact waiver they are pursuing. It can be especially helpful to ask whether the program has an application process, an interest list, a waitlist, or a separate eligibility screen.

We need to know if a parent can be paid

Families may want to begin with the exact program responsible for the service, such as the waiver administrator, Medicaid agency, or managed care plan. The best first call can vary depending on whether the service is provided through a waiver, a state plan benefit, or a self-directed model.

School is not helping

Families may want to begin with the child’s school team, special education contact, or district representative, depending on the issue. If that does not resolve the problem, Parent Centers, advocates, or state-specific disability-rights resources may be useful next steps. Procedures can vary by state, district, and school system.

We need legal or advocacy help

Families may want to begin with the Protection and Advocacy agency in their state, a Parent Center, a disability-rights organization, or another qualified advocacy group depending on the issue. The best first call can vary depending on whether the problem involves school, benefits, access, neglect, discrimination, or service denials.

We are planning for adulthood

Families may want to begin with the adult-service side of the current system, the school transition team, Medicaid, a support coordinator, or planning resources related to benefits, housing, and long-term supports. A practical first step is often to identify which systems will change at adulthood and which records need to be gathered early.

We are overwhelmed and do not know where to start

Families may want to begin with the clearest immediate problem rather than trying to solve everything at once. A pediatrician, care manager, hospital social worker, Family-to-Family Health Information Center, Parent Center, or diagnosis-specific organization may be a useful first step when a family needs help sorting out which system to contact next.

Waitlists and interest lists

Waitlists and interest lists are one of the most confusing parts of disability and Medicaid systems. Many families assume that qualifying for a program and receiving services are the same thing, but that is not always how these systems work. This page explains the difference in plain English and highlights why timing can matter so much.

Medicaid eligibility is not always the same as waiver access

A person may qualify for Medicaid and still not have access to a specific waiver or long-term-support program right away. Medicaid eligibility answers one question. Waiver access may involve a separate program, additional screening, limited program capacity, or a waitlist.

Being eligible is not always the same as receiving services

A person may be told that they are eligible for a program or that they appear to meet the criteria, but services may still not begin immediately. In some systems, there may be a delay before a slot opens, before a provider is available, or before the next step in the process can move forward.

What an interest list is

An interest list is often a way for a state or program to record that a person wants a service or waiver that may not be immediately available. In some systems, getting on the interest list is an important early step even if the full application or final eligibility process happens later.

What a waitlist is

A waitlist usually means the person is waiting for access to a limited program, service slot, or next stage of service approval. The exact meaning can vary by program or state. Available public materials do not always describe waitlists and interest lists in the same way across systems.

Why some programs move slowly

Programs may move slowly because of limited funding, restricted waiver slots, staffing shortages, provider shortages, reassessment timing, or administrative backlogs. Local implementation can matter too. Two families in the same state may have very different experiences depending on region, provider availability, and the exact program involved.

Why families may need to get on a list early

For some waiver or disability-support systems, getting on the list early can be one of the most important practical steps a family takes. Waiting until a crisis happens can make it harder to access a program in time if the program already has a long queue.

Why a long wait does not always mean the family is in the wrong program

A long wait does not necessarily mean the family has chosen the wrong pathway. In some states, long delays are part of how the system functions for certain programs. Families may still want to verify that they are in the correct program and ask whether any related services, interim supports, or parallel pathways are available.

Why direct verification matters

Waitlist and interest-list rules can change. Some programs use formal waitlists, some use interest lists, and some use different regional or category-based processes. Families should verify current details directly with the state, waiver program, or agency involved rather than relying only on older descriptions or informal advice.

Practical next step

When a family is pursuing a waiver or long-term-support program, it may be helpful to ask three direct questions: Is there a waitlist or interest list, am I already on it, and what happens next after that step? Those answers can make the system easier to understand and can help families plan more realistically. Families comparing programs may also want to review the State Directory for state-specific starting points.

Important reminder

This page is designed as a practical explanation, not a guarantee of how any one program works. These systems vary by state, region, and program. Families should verify current details directly with the state or program involved and ask how local provider shortages or regional implementation may affect timing.

Therapies and supports

This section offers a plain-English overview of common therapies families may hear about in medical, school, early intervention, and disability-service systems. It is designed to help families understand what these therapies generally focus on, what families may hear about them, and what current research does or does not clearly show. This section is intended as general educational information and is not medical advice. For some therapies, more research may still be needed.

Physical therapy

Physical therapy, often called PT, generally focuses on movement, strength, balance, mobility, positioning, transfers, endurance, and physical access to daily activities. Families may hear about physical therapy when a child or adult has mobility challenges, weakness, motor delays, coordination concerns, or needs support with walking, equipment use, or safe movement.

Occupational therapy

Occupational therapy, often called OT, generally focuses on daily activities and functional skills. This can include fine motor skills, hand use, dressing, feeding, sensory regulation, positioning, self-care routines, school participation, and access to adaptive tools. Families may hear about occupational therapy when the goal is to improve daily independence or make everyday tasks more manageable.

Speech-language therapy

Speech-language therapy can support more than spoken speech. It may also focus on language, understanding, social communication, feeding, swallowing, and alternative forms of communication. Families may hear about speech therapy when a person has delays or challenges related to communication, comprehension, articulation, oral motor function, or feeding-related needs.

Feeding therapy

Feeding therapy is often used when a child or adult has difficulty with eating, chewing, swallowing, oral motor coordination, food tolerance, or safe mealtime participation. Depending on the setting, feeding-related support may come through speech therapy, occupational therapy, or a specialized feeding team.

Behavioral therapy

Behavioral therapy is a broad term that can describe different approaches used to support behavior, routines, regulation, skill-building, and participation in daily life. Families may hear about behavioral supports when a child or adult is struggling with transitions, safety, communication, self-regulation, or participation across home, school, or community settings.

Mental health therapy

Mental health therapy may include counseling, psychotherapy, family therapy, or other forms of emotional and behavioral support. Families may hear about these services when a person needs support related to anxiety, depression, trauma, adjustment, emotional regulation, caregiver stress, or broader mental health concerns.

Developmental therapy

Developmental therapy is often discussed in early childhood and early intervention systems. It may focus on broad developmental skills such as play, learning, interaction, attention, problem-solving, and participation in age-appropriate activities. The exact meaning can vary by program or state.

Vision and hearing-related therapy or support

Some children and adults may also receive orientation and mobility services, vision services, hearing-related services, or communication supports related to hearing loss. Families may hear about these supports when access, safety, communication, and participation are affected by vision or hearing differences.

Aquatic, hippotherapy, and other specialized therapies

Families may also hear about therapies delivered in specialized settings, such as aquatic therapy or horse-related therapy programs. These services may be helpful for some people depending on goals, access, safety, and clinician recommendations, but coverage and availability vary widely. Families should verify current program details directly with the provider, insurer, or program involved.

MRNI

MNRI, also called the Masgutova method or Masgutova Neurosensorimotor Reflex Integration, is an approach centered on primitive reflex patterns and reflex integration. Families may hear about it when looking for neurodevelopmental therapies for motor, sensory, regulation, learning, or reflex-related concerns.

Some clinics and training programs describe MRNI as a way to support reflex integration, nervous-system organization, and broader function. Current research is still limited. Available studies are relatively small, and stronger independent research is still needed before strong conclusions can be drawn about effectiveness across diagnoses and outcomes.

MRNI is generally described as a hands-on therapy approach, but families should still discuss it with their medical and therapy team, especially when a child has complex medical needs. Some larger specialty providers and training organizations offer this approach, and families may also encounter it through private clinics or intensive programs. Availability varies by provider and location.

DMI

DMI, or Dynamic Movement Intervention, is a therapy approach used by some physical and occupational therapists to provoke movement responses and work on postural control, alignment, and motor skills. Families may hear about it most often in pediatric neurological and developmental therapy settings.

Some larger therapy centers, including NAPA Center, describe DMI as a way to support developmental progress and gross motor function. Current research is still limited. Families may hear strong positive descriptions from providers and other families, but there has not been enough high-quality research to draw strong conclusions yet about how DMI compares with more established approaches across different diagnoses.

DMI is usually encountered through specialty pediatric therapy clinics and intensive therapy centers. It is not generally described as invasive, but it can be physically demanding and may not be appropriate for every child in every stage of care. Families should discuss fit, goals, and safety with their therapy team before making decisions.

Red light therapy

Red light therapy usually refers to low-level light or laser-based treatment that is marketed for different goals, including pain, inflammation, tissue healing, muscle recovery, or neurological support. Families may hear about it through rehabilitation clinics, wellness providers, or specialty therapy centers.

The perceived benefit often depends on the condition being discussed. Research may be more developed for some uses than for others. For pediatric neurological rehabilitation and disability-related functional goals, research is still limited. Some studies and reviews suggest promise in narrow areas, but current evidence does not clearly show broad established benefit across the kinds of developmental and disability-related goals many families are asking about.

Red light therapy is often described as noninvasive, but families should still speak with their medical team first, especially when a child has seizures, eye-related concerns, complex medical needs, or when a provider is making broad claims that go beyond the available evidence. Families may encounter this therapy through private clinics, wellness centers, and some rehabilitation providers.

Chiropractic care

Chiropractic care usually centers on spinal manipulation and other manual techniques, often with the goal of improving pain, alignment, mobility, or overall function. Families may hear about chiropractic care for musculoskeletal concerns, posture, comfort, or broader neurological or developmental claims.

The evidence depends heavily on the condition being treated. For some adult musculoskeletal pain concerns, research may be more developed. For pediatric disability-related neurological and developmental goals, evidence is much less clear. Current evidence does not clearly support broad claims about chiropractic care improving complex developmental or neurological conditions, and stronger research is still needed in many areas.

Because spinal manipulation is involved, chiropractic care is not the same as low-risk supportive stretching or positioning work. Families should discuss it with their medical team first, especially for children with complex medical needs, spinal concerns, seizure disorders, bone fragility, or significant neurological conditions. Some larger therapy or wellness settings may offer related services, but appropriateness depends heavily on the individual and the condition being treated.

Stem cell therapy

Stem cell therapy is a broad term that can refer to different types of cell-based treatments. Families may hear about it in the context of cerebral palsy, neurological injury, rare disease, or developmental disability, often through research programs, international clinics, or private marketing.

The perceived benefit is often framed around repair, regeneration, neuroprotection, or improved function. Some research in conditions such as cerebral palsy is promising and continues to develop, but the field is still evolving. Stronger research is still needed to understand which cell types, methods, doses, timing, and patient groups may actually benefit. This is especially important because provider marketing can sound much more certain than the current evidence supports.

Stem cell therapy requires especially careful review. Some uses remain experimental or are being studied in research settings, while some marketed clinical offerings may not be approved or may not be supported by strong evidence. Families should discuss this with their medical team and carefully verify regulation, oversight, safety, and evidence before making decisions. Some families may encounter this therapy through research centers, international clinics, or private regenerative-medicine providers. Availability and oversight vary widely.

How families may hear about newer or specialized therapies

Families may hear about newer or specialized therapies through therapy centers, provider recommendations, social media, parent networks, conferences, or private clinics. In some cases, a therapy may be offered by a large or well-known center, but that does not by itself show that the therapy has strong research support.

A helpful approach is to separate three questions: what the therapy claims to do, what goals the family hopes to address, and what current evidence actually supports. Some families report benefit from therapies that still have limited research, but stronger studies may still be needed to know how consistently those benefits occur and for whom.

How families may hear about therapy options

Therapies may be recommended through hospitals, pediatricians, specialists, school evaluations, early intervention, waiver programs, or community providers. In some cases the same therapy may look different depending on the setting. School-based therapy, outpatient therapy, and home-based therapy may all serve different purposes.

How to think about whether a therapy may be useful

A helpful starting point is to ask what the therapy is trying to improve, support, or make safer. Families may find it helpful to think in terms of practical goals such as communication, eating, movement, participation, regulation, comfort, school access, or daily living skills rather than focusing only on the therapy title.

Important reminder

This section is intended as a general educational overview. It should not replace individualized medical, therapy, or developmental guidance. Therapy recommendations, goals, and coverage rules can vary by state, insurer, school system, provider, diagnosis, and age. Families should verify current recommendations and program details directly with the professionals and systems involved. For some therapies, more research may still be needed.

Printable checklists

These print-friendly tools are designed to give families a simple starting point for paperwork, hospital visits, and waiver preparation. They are meant to be practical reference pages and may be printed directly from the browser.

Paperwork checklist

Keep copies of diagnosis records, evaluations, prescriptions, letters of medical necessity, Medicaid notices, denial letters, prior authorization records, school records, insurance correspondence, and key contact information. Families may find it helpful to keep the most recent records near the front and store deadlines in one easy-to-find place.

Hospital and emergency planning sheet

Keep a current list of diagnoses, medications, allergies, specialists, equipment, communication needs, emergency instructions, and discharge contacts. Families may find it helpful to update this sheet regularly so it is ready if an urgent visit or hospital stay happens unexpectedly.

Benefits and waiver preparation checklist

Before applying, gather recent medical records, therapy notes, functional assessments, school documents, financial and eligibility paperwork, names of current providers, and any prior Medicaid or waiver correspondence. Families may also want to confirm whether there is an application, an interest list, a waitlist, or a separate screening step.

Glossary

This glossary explains common disability, Medicaid, school-support, and planning terms in plain English. Some terms are used differently across programs, states, insurers, and school systems. When a term affects eligibility, benefits, or legal rights, families should verify current program details directly with the relevant agency, school system, insurer, or organization.

Medicaid

Medicaid is a public health coverage program that helps pay for health care and, in many cases, long-term services and supports for eligible people. The exact rules and covered services can vary by state.

Medicaid waiver

A Medicaid waiver is a program that allows a state to offer certain services or use certain rules that differ from standard Medicaid. Waivers are often used for long-term supports, home-based services, and disability-specific service systems.

HCBS

HCBS stands for home and community-based services. These are services designed to help people receive support in their home or community instead of in an institution or other more restrictive setting.

Level of care

Level of care is a program standard used to decide how much support a person needs. In Medicaid systems, it often refers to whether the person’s needs are serious enough to qualify for certain long-term service programs or waivers.

Institutional level of care

Institutional level of care usually means the person’s needs are significant enough that they could qualify for care in a hospital, nursing facility, or other institutional setting. Many waiver programs use this standard as part of eligibility.

Medically necessary

Medically necessary usually means a service, treatment, or item is considered needed for diagnosis, treatment, safety, or functioning under the rules of the program or insurer involved. The exact meaning can vary by program, insurer, or state.

Prior authorization

Prior authorization is approval that may be required before a service, treatment, or piece of equipment will be covered. A request is usually submitted with medical records or other supporting documentation.

Appeal

An appeal is a request for a decision to be reviewed again after a denial, reduction, or other negative decision. Appeal procedures can vary by state, insurer, and program.

Fair hearing

A fair hearing is a more formal review process used in many public-benefit systems, including Medicaid. It is generally different from an informal complaint or phone call and may involve a hearing officer or designated reviewer.

Eligibility

Eligibility means whether a person meets the rules for a program, benefit, or service. Eligibility can depend on factors such as disability, age, income, resources, level of care, diagnosis, and state rules.

Spend-down

Spend-down is a rule used in some Medicaid-related systems where a person may qualify only after certain income or medical-expense thresholds are met. The exact meaning can vary by state and category of coverage.

Self-direction

Self-direction is a service model that may give the person receiving services, or their family or representative, more control over how certain supports are arranged. In some programs, it can affect who provides care and how services are managed.

Consumer-directed services

Consumer-directed services are services that allow the person receiving support, or a representative, to have more choice over workers, scheduling, or service management. Programs do not all use this term in the same way.

Case manager

A case manager is a person who helps coordinate services, benefits, referrals, or care planning. The exact role can vary widely by program and agency.

Support coordinator

A support coordinator is a person who helps organize services and service plans, often in waiver or developmental-disability systems. Some programs use this title instead of case manager.

Respite

Respite is short-term relief care for a family caregiver or primary support person. It may be offered through Medicaid waivers, state programs, nonprofits, or local agencies.

Durable medical equipment

Durable medical equipment, often called DME, includes medical or adaptive equipment that is meant to be used over time, such as wheelchairs, hospital beds, feeding equipment, or other supportive devices.

IEP

IEP stands for Individualized Education Program. It is a written special education plan for an eligible student with a disability and helps guide the student’s educational services and supports.

IFSP

IFSP stands for Individualized Family Service Plan. It is generally used in early intervention for eligible infants and toddlers and focuses on the child’s development and family-centered supports.

504 plan

A 504 plan is a school-based accommodation plan used to help a student with a disability access education. It is different from an IEP and is often used when accommodations or access supports are needed.

SSI

SSI stands for Supplemental Security Income. It is a federal program that provides monthly payments to eligible people who are aged, blind, or disabled and who have limited income and resources.

SSDI

SSDI stands for Social Security Disability Insurance. It is a federal disability-benefit program tied to work history or certain related benefit structures under Social Security.

ABLE account

An ABLE account is a tax-advantaged savings and investment account for certain people with disabilities. It is designed to help pay for qualified disability expenses while preserving access to many means-tested benefits in many situations.

Special needs trust

A special needs trust is a legal arrangement that can hold money or property for the benefit of a person with a disability in a way that may help preserve eligibility for certain means-tested benefits. The exact effect depends on the trust structure and the program involved.

Medically complex

Medically complex usually refers to a child or adult with significant health needs, often involving multiple conditions, technology dependence, frequent specialty care, or ongoing high-level medical support. Programs do not all define this term in the same way.

Developmental disability

Developmental disability generally refers to a disability that begins during the developmental period and affects major life areas such as learning, mobility, communication, independent living, or other daily functioning. The exact meaning can vary by program or state.

Important reminder

This glossary is designed to make common terms easier to understand. It is not a legal dictionary, and some terms are used differently across states, school systems, insurers, and benefit programs. Families should verify current program details directly with the relevant agency or organization when a definition affects eligibility, services, or rights.

Care notebook and paperwork toolkit

Keeping records organized can make disability systems easier to navigate. Families often have to use the same documents across medical care, therapy, school services, Medicaid, equipment requests, denials, appeals, and waiver applications. A clear notebook, binder, or digital folder can help reduce stress and make it easier to respond when something urgent comes up.

Diagnosis records

Keep copies of formal diagnoses, specialist reports, developmental assessments, and any summary documents that explain the person’s condition. These records often become the foundation for later applications, evaluations, and service requests.

Therapy evaluations

Physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, feeding therapy, behavioral therapy, and other evaluations can help show functional need. These records are often useful for school services, equipment requests, waiver applications, and medical necessity documentation.

Prescriptions

Prescriptions are often required for equipment, therapies, supplies, and some home-based services. It can help to keep both current prescriptions and older prescriptions that may still support a pattern of need.

Letters of medical necessity

Letters of medical necessity are often central to equipment requests, skilled nursing requests, home modifications, and appeals. Keeping copies of submitted letters can help families reuse strong supporting language when a similar request comes up later.

Medicaid notices

Approval letters, renewal notices, eligibility notices, waiver notices, and managed care letters should all be kept together. These documents often contain deadlines, reference numbers, coverage details, and decisions that become important later.

Denial letters

Denial letters should be saved in full, including all pages. These notices often explain the reason for the denial, the date of the decision, the deadline for appeal, and the process for requesting review or a hearing.

Prior authorization records

Keep copies of prior authorization requests, supporting attachments, and any response letters. These records can help show whether an item or service was previously requested, what documentation was used, and what may need to be corrected or strengthened in the future.

Waiver letters and program notices

Waitlist notices, interest list letters, waiver approvals, service plan notices, reassessment letters, and program correspondence should be saved in one place. These records often help families understand status, timing, and next steps across long-term support systems.

IEP and IFSP documents

School and early intervention records can be important outside of education settings as well. IEPs, IFSPs, evaluations, progress reports, and meeting notes often help show functional need, service history, and areas of support required in daily life.

Specialist contact information

It can help to keep a current list of doctors, therapists, case managers, service coordinators, equipment vendors, social workers, school contacts, and program staff. A simple contact sheet can save time during urgent calls, admissions, denials, or renewals.

Hospital discharge paperwork

Discharge summaries, medication instructions, care recommendations, and follow-up plans can be important for home nursing requests, equipment requests, therapy authorization, and waiver screening. These records often show why home supports are needed right away.

Assessment results

Functional assessments, level-of-care reviews, nursing assessments, developmental assessments, and behavioral evaluations can be especially important in Medicaid and waiver systems. Keeping copies makes it easier to understand how the person was evaluated and what criteria were applied.

Insurance correspondence

Insurance letters, explanation of benefits documents, call summaries, case notes, and coverage notices should be saved together. These records may become important if a family needs to compare insurance and Medicaid responses or appeal a denial.

Financial and eligibility documents

Some programs require proof of income, resources, guardianship status, residency, identity, and household information. Keeping these materials organized can make applications and renewals less stressful and reduce delays caused by missing paperwork.

Call log or communication tracker

A simple log can be one of the most useful tools a family keeps. It can include the date, the name of the person spoken to, the phone number, the program or agency, a summary of what was said, any reference number given, and the next expected step.

How to keep it manageable

A care notebook does not need to be perfect. Some families prefer a binder with labeled sections. Others prefer digital folders by topic or by year. What matters most is that records can be found quickly when a new application, denial, school meeting, or equipment issue comes up.

Families may find it helpful to organize the notebook by category, keep the most recent documents near the front, and store important deadlines and phone numbers in one easy-to-find place.

Why this helps

Many disability systems ask for the same information repeatedly. Having records organized can reduce stress, improve response time, and make it easier to show patterns of need over time. It can also help families prepare for school meetings, Medicaid renewals, denials, and appeals with less scrambling.

Important reminder

This page is meant to help families stay organized. The documents that matter most may vary by state, program, diagnosis, insurer, and level of need. Families should verify current program requirements directly with the agency, insurer, school system, or provider involved.

Special needs trusts

A special needs trust is a legal arrangement designed to hold money or property for the benefit of a person with a disability in a way that may help preserve eligibility for means-tested public benefits such as Supplemental Security Income and Medicaid. Trusts are governed by state law, and the way a trust affects benefits can also depend on federal benefit rules and program-specific requirements.

This page is a general information resource. It is meant to help families understand the basic structure of special needs trusts and the questions they may want to ask. It is not legal, tax, or financial advice. Because trust and Medicaid rules can vary by state, families should verify current program details directly with a qualified attorney and the relevant state agency before relying on a specific plan.

What a special needs trust is

A special needs trust is a trust created for the benefit of a person with a disability. In general, the trust is managed by a trustee, and the beneficiary does not receive direct ownership or direct control of the trust assets in the same way they would if the money were given to them outright.

That structure can matter because many means-tested public benefit programs place limits on countable income and resources. A properly structured special needs trust may allow assets to be held and used for the person’s benefit without those assets automatically being treated the same way as money held directly by the beneficiary.

Why families use them

Families often use special needs trusts to help protect funds for a disabled person’s future while also trying to preserve access to important public benefits. The trust can allow a trustee to manage funds over time for the beneficiary’s needs instead of distributing a large sum directly to the person.

These trusts are often used in estate planning, settlement planning, and long-term support planning. They can also help coordinate support when a person may need both private financial help and public benefits at the same time.

Why the money is meant to work for the beneficiary

The trust exists for the benefit of the disabled person. In practice, that means the assets are held, managed, and used to support that person rather than being distributed directly as unrestricted cash or used for someone else’s benefit.

Exactly how this works depends on the trust language, the trustee’s authority, the source of the funds, and the public benefits involved. Available public materials do not always describe every practical spending rule the same way, so families should verify current program details directly with qualified counsel and the relevant agencies.

First-party and third-party special needs trusts

The most important difference between first-party and third-party special needs trusts is where the money came from.

A first-party special needs trust is funded with the disabled person’s own assets. This may include funds the person received directly, such as a settlement, inheritance already paid to them, back benefits, or other assets legally belonging to the beneficiary.

A third-party special needs trust is funded with assets that never belonged to the disabled person. These trusts are commonly used by parents, grandparents, or other family members who want to set aside money for the person’s future through their own estate planning or lifetime gifts.

Why the distinction matters

The distinction matters because first-party and third-party trusts are treated differently under benefit rules and under trust planning. The source of the funds can affect who may create the trust, how the trust must be structured, and what happens to any funds left in the trust at the beneficiary’s death.

That difference is especially important when families are deciding how to leave an inheritance, whether settlement funds should be placed in trust, or whether a trust should be created during life or through an estate plan.

What a special needs trust can be used for

In general, special needs trusts are used to supplement, not replace, public benefits. Depending on the trust terms and the benefit rules involved, trust funds may be used for items or services that improve quality of life, support health and safety, or help meet needs not fully covered by public benefits.

Exactly what should or should not be paid from the trust can depend on the benefit program involved, the trust type, the state, and the specific expense. For that reason, spending decisions should be reviewed carefully. Families should verify current program details directly with the trustee, qualified counsel, and the relevant agency before relying on a specific use of trust funds.

Can a special needs trust buy a home?

In some situations, a special needs trust may be able to purchase a home for the benefit of the disabled person if there are enough funds in the trust and the trust terms allow it. This can be a meaningful planning tool for long-term housing stability, but it requires careful review.

Home ownership through a special needs trust can raise important questions about who owns the property, how housing-related expenses are handled, how public benefits may be affected, and what happens if the property is sold later. Because these issues can be highly technical, families should verify current program details directly with a qualified attorney and the relevant benefit programs before using trust funds to purchase a home.

First-party trust Medicaid payback

One of the most important differences between first-party and third-party special needs trusts is what may happen to funds remaining at death. Under federal law, a first-party special needs trust that relies on the federal exception for a disabled individual’s own assets generally must provide that the state will receive amounts remaining in the trust, up to the total amount of medical assistance paid on behalf of the beneficiary under the state Medicaid plan.

This is often described as Medicaid payback or Medicaid reimbursement. It is a major planning issue and one reason families are often advised to avoid having assets pass directly to the disabled person when a third-party planning option is available.

Third-party trust and Medicaid payback

A third-party special needs trust is different because it is funded with someone else’s assets, not the beneficiary’s own funds. As a general planning matter, third-party special needs trusts are often used specifically because they do not follow the same Medicaid payback structure as first-party trusts.

Even so, trust drafting, administration, and state law still matter. Families should verify current trust terms and state-specific rules directly with qualified counsel before making planning decisions.

Why state rules still matter

Special needs trusts are regulated by state law, and benefit administration can also vary by program and by state. That means trust validity, trustee duties, notice requirements, Medicaid treatment, and related planning rules may not look exactly the same in every state.

This is similar to Medicaid more broadly. Federal rules matter, but state-specific rules and administration can change how a trust works in practice. Families should verify current program details directly with the state and with a qualified attorney in the state whose law applies to the trust.

Common planning questions

Families often want to know whether a trust should be first-party or third-party, who should serve as trustee, what expenses can safely be paid, whether the trust can hold or buy a home, and what effect the trust may have on SSI and Medicaid.

Those questions are important, but the correct answer depends on the source of the funds, the beneficiary’s benefits, the trust language, and the law of the relevant state. Available public materials do not always describe those issues in a way that fits every situation, so individualized legal guidance is often important.

Important reminder

This page is intended as a general educational overview. It should not be treated as legal, tax, financial, Medicaid-planning, or estate-planning advice. Special needs trusts are technical legal tools, and the wrong structure or the wrong use of trust funds can have serious consequences for benefits and long-term planning.

Families should verify current program details directly with a qualified attorney and the relevant state or benefit agency before creating, funding, administering, or spending from a special needs trust.

ABLE accounts

An ABLE account is a tax-advantaged savings and investment account for certain people with disabilities. These accounts were created under Section 529A of the tax code and are designed to help eligible individuals save for qualified disability expenses while preserving access to many means-tested benefits. Families should verify current eligibility rules, contribution limits, and plan details directly with official sources before relying on a specific account strategy.

What an ABLE account is

ABLE accounts are special savings and investment accounts for eligible people with disabilities. They are designed to allow money to be set aside for disability-related expenses in a way that can be more flexible than some other planning tools.

Official federal materials describe ABLE accounts as tax-advantaged accounts that can be used for qualified disability expenses. These accounts can be an important part of day-to-day financial planning and longer-term independence planning.

Who may be eligible

Eligibility is tied to disability onset rules under federal law. Current official materials explain that ABLE eligibility includes people whose disability began before age 46. Families should verify current eligibility details directly with the official ABLE program materials or the ABLE National Resource Center, especially because federal rules have changed over time.

Why families use ABLE accounts

Families often use ABLE accounts because they can provide a practical place to save money for disability-related expenses while preserving access to important public benefits in many situations. They may also help a person with a disability have more direct access to funds for everyday needs connected to disability and independent living.

How ABLE accounts differ from special needs trusts

ABLE accounts and special needs trusts can both be important tools, but they are not the same. A special needs trust is a legal trust arrangement managed by a trustee under trust law. An ABLE account is a tax-advantaged savings and investment account created under federal law and opened through a state ABLE program.

ABLE accounts may be easier to use for some routine expenses and more direct day-to-day spending. Special needs trusts may be more useful for larger amounts of money, more complex planning, or situations that require trustee oversight. Many families use both tools together rather than choosing only one. Families who want a simpler first step may also want to review the ABLE Accounts section alongside this page.

What ABLE funds can generally support

Federal materials describe ABLE funds as available for qualified disability expenses. In general, those expenses can include a wide range of disability-related needs connected to health, housing, education, transportation, assistive technology, personal support, and other areas that help support health, independence, or quality of life.

Even though the federal framework is broad, families should verify current program details directly with the relevant state ABLE plan and official tax guidance before relying on a specific use of funds.

Why ABLE accounts can matter for independence

ABLE accounts can help families plan for expenses without requiring every dollar to pass through a more formal trust structure. For some people, that can make it easier to manage smaller or recurring disability-related expenses, build savings habits, and create more day-to-day financial flexibility.

Contribution limits and state plans

Contribution limits apply, and official federal materials explain that the annual contribution amount can change over time. State ABLE plans also differ in fees, investment choices, residency rules, and account features. Some state plans accept out-of-state residents, while others may not.

Families should verify current contribution limits, state plan rules, fees, and features directly with the state ABLE plan and official sources before opening or funding an account.

Important reminder

This page is a general educational overview. It should not be treated as tax, legal, estate-planning, or benefits-planning advice. ABLE rules can change, and the effect of an ABLE account can depend on the person’s benefits, income, and financial situation.

SSI and SSDI basics

Supplemental Security Income and Social Security Disability Insurance are two different disability benefit programs administered by the Social Security Administration. Families often hear both names at the same time, which can make them easy to confuse. Understanding the difference can help families ask better questions and prepare for the right kind of application or planning conversation.

What SSI is

SSI stands for Supplemental Security Income. Official Social Security materials describe SSI as a program that provides monthly payments to people with disabilities, blindness, or older age who have limited income and resources. SSI can be especially important for disabled children and adults with low income and limited assets.

What SSDI is

SSDI stands for Social Security Disability Insurance. Official Social Security materials describe SSDI as a program that provides monthly payments to people who have a disability and enough work history in jobs covered by Social Security. In broad terms, SSDI is tied to work and insurance status rather than only to financial need.

Why people often confuse them

Both programs use disability standards, both are run through Social Security, and both can matter for long-term financial stability and health coverage. Even so, they are different programs with different financial rules, different eligibility structures, and different benefit pathways.

Why SSI matters for many families

SSI is especially relevant for many disabled children and adults with limited income and resources. In many situations, SSI can also matter because it may connect to Medicaid eligibility or serve as part of a broader benefits plan, depending on the state and the person’s circumstances.

Why SSDI matters

SSDI can matter because it may provide monthly income for a disabled adult who has enough work history or who qualifies through a related benefit structure. Official federal materials also explain that people receiving SSDI generally become eligible for Medicare after the required waiting period, with limited exceptions such as ALS and certain kidney-related situations described in official materials.

Why the distinction matters in planning

The difference between SSI and SSDI can affect monthly income, health coverage, resource rules, and future planning. Families may find it helpful to ask whether the person’s likely path is based on limited income and resources, work history, or another related Social Security structure.

Important reminder

SSI and SSDI rules can be technical, and the effect on Medicaid, Medicare, or related benefits can vary by program and by state. Families should verify current rules directly with the Social Security Administration and with any related state benefit agency before relying on a specific benefit assumption.

School supports

School systems are often a major part of disability support planning. Even when a child’s biggest needs are medical, early intervention records, evaluations, IEPs, 504 plans, and transition documents can play an important role in understanding support needs over time. This section is a general information resource and is not legal advice. Procedures can vary by state, district, and school system, and families should verify current procedures directly with their school district, state education agency, or qualified advocate.

Early intervention and IFSPs

Early intervention services are designed for eligible infants and toddlers with disabilities and their families. These services are different from school-age special education and are part of the early childhood support system rather than the K-12 school system.

An IFSP, or Individualized Family Service Plan, is generally used in early intervention. It is family-centered and focuses on the child’s development and the services and supports needed in the early years. Early records can matter later because they may help document developmental history, service needs, and patterns of support over time.

IEP basics

An IEP, or Individualized Education Program, is a written plan developed for an eligible student with a disability. It guides the delivery of special education and related services and is one of the central tools used to support the student’s educational program.

An IEP often includes the student’s present levels, goals, special education services, related services, supports, and other key planning details. Families may find it helpful to keep copies of evaluations, service pages, goals, progress reports, and meeting notes because those records can become important later.

504 plans

A 504 plan is often used when a student with a disability needs accommodations, related aids, services, or access support even if the student is not receiving special education through an IEP.

A 504 plan can matter even when a child’s needs are mostly medical, behavioral, health-related, or access-related. In those situations, accommodations and related supports may still be important even if the child does not have an IEP.

Evaluations and reevaluations

Evaluations matter because they help determine eligibility, services, accommodations, and educational needs. Reevaluations can also matter over time when a student’s needs change, when progress needs to be reviewed, or when the family and school are reassessing the right support structure.

Families may want to request written reports and keep copies of evaluations because school records can be useful outside of school systems too. They may support broader disability documentation, waiver conversations, equipment requests, or transition planning. Available public materials do not always describe local implementation in the same way, so local practice should be verified directly with the school system involved.

School transition planning

Transition planning in school generally refers to planning that helps a student move toward adult life, including employment, education, training, community participation, and other post-school goals.

Transition planning can connect school services to adulthood, employment supports, community life, and service systems outside school. Families may find it helpful to start early so planning does not become rushed as a student gets older.

Why school records matter beyond school

IEPs, IFSPs, evaluations, and other school records can help document communication needs, mobility needs, cognitive or developmental needs, daily support needs, and service history. That information may help support waiver applications, equipment requests, disability documentation, transition planning, and broader service coordination.

School records can still matter even when a child’s biggest challenges are medical. They may provide some of the clearest written records of how disability affects daily functioning, communication, learning, behavior, and participation over time.

Practical school support resources

Parent Centers are one of the most practical national starting points for families who need help understanding school systems, IEPs, 504 plans, evaluations, and special education meetings. Parent Training and Information Centers and Community Parent Resource Centers serve families of children and youth with disabilities across the country.

The U.S. Department of Education’s IDEA resources and Section 504 resources are also strong federal reference points when families want official baseline information about rights, procedures, and school responsibilities.

Why families often need both school and service-system support

School supports and disability-service systems often overlap, but they do not serve the same purpose. Families may need both educational supports and separate Medicaid, waiver, therapy, or medical systems at the same time. Understanding school rights can still be important even when the biggest daily pressures involve medical care, equipment, or long-term supports.

Procedures can vary by state, district, and school system, and families should verify current procedures directly with their school district, state education agency, or qualified advocate before relying on a specific process or timeline.

Transition to adulthood

Turning 18 can be one of the biggest transition points in disability systems. Benefits, decision-making rules, school planning, service eligibility, and long-term support structures may all begin to shift around the late teen years. This page provides a broad national overview to help families plan ahead.

Why age 18 matters

In many systems, age 18 marks a legal and administrative transition point. That does not mean every benefit or service changes immediately, but it often means families begin hearing new terms, new planning questions, and different rules about decision-making, eligibility, and adult service systems.

Adult Medicaid and adult service systems

Child-serving systems and adult-serving systems are not always structured the same way. Some programs end, some change categories, and some require a new application or reassessment. Families may want to begin asking about adult pathways well before the person turns 18.

Medical decision-making questions

As a disabled young adult approaches age 18, families often begin hearing about decision-making options, consent, privacy, and related legal planning questions. The right approach depends on the individual, the state, and the person’s abilities and support needs.

School transition planning

School transition planning can be a key part of preparing for adulthood. This may include planning around postsecondary education, employment, community participation, daily living skills, and connections to adult agencies or services.

Benefits changes

Benefits planning may become more complicated during the late teen years. SSI, SSDI-related questions, Medicaid categories, adult waiver pathways, and financial-planning tools may all become more relevant during this stage.

Planning ahead

Families often find it helpful to begin planning before age 18 rather than waiting until services or rules change. Gathering records, learning the adult system, and identifying likely future needs can make the transition feel more manageable.

Important reminder

Transition timelines and adult-system rules can vary by state, program, school district, and diagnosis. Families should verify current program details directly with the state, school system, Medicaid agency, and any adult-service program involved.

Housing and adult life planning

Housing and adult life planning can involve many different systems, and no one model fits every person. Families often hear about supported living, in-home supports, shared living arrangements, day programs, transportation supports, and employment services all at once. This page offers a broad national framework for thinking about those options.

Supported living

Supported living can describe a range of arrangements where a disabled adult lives in the community with some level of support. The structure, staffing, and service model can vary significantly by state and program.

In-home supports

Some adults continue living with family or in their own home while receiving in-home supports. These may include personal care, direct support staff, nursing-related services, or other long-term supports depending on the person’s needs and program eligibility.

Group or shared living settings

Families may hear about group homes, shared living, host homes, or other supervised or semi-supervised housing models. The level of structure, independence, and staffing can vary widely, so it can be helpful to ask detailed questions about how a specific setting actually works.

Day programs and community participation

Adult life planning often includes more than housing. Families may also need to think about day programs, community-based activities, skill-building, or structured daytime supports, depending on the person’s goals and support needs.

Employment supports

Employment may be part of adult planning for some people, and employment supports can vary from job coaching to vocational rehabilitation to broader support planning. The right path depends on the individual’s goals, skills, and support needs.

Transportation

Transportation is often one of the most important parts of adult life planning. Work, community participation, medical care, and daily routines can all depend on reliable transportation supports, which may come from different systems than housing or waiver services.

Why housing and services are not always the same

One of the most important planning points is that housing and services are often connected, but they are not always the same thing. A person may have a place to live but still need separate services to remain safe and supported there. In other situations, a service system may be identified before a stable housing option is in place.

Why early planning helps

Housing and adult life planning often work best when families begin early. Early planning can make it easier to understand waitlists, adult service systems, employment supports, transportation needs, and financial planning tools before the transition becomes urgent.

Important reminder

Available local options can vary widely by state, county, provider network, diagnosis, and funding system. Families should verify current local options directly with the state, provider organizations, adult-service agencies, and support coordinators involved.

Denials and appeals

This page is a general national information resource for families and caregivers. It is designed to help people understand common denial and appeal issues in disability, Medicaid, and insurance systems. It is not legal advice, and exact rules, forms, and deadlines can vary by state, program, managed care plan, and insurer.

What a denial letter usually means

A denial letter usually means a program, insurer, or agency has decided not to approve a service, item, payment, or eligibility request in the form that was requested. In some cases, the denial may be complete. In other cases, it may mean the request was approved only in part, reduced, delayed, or routed into a different category than the family expected.

Denial letters can be confusing because they often use technical language. Even so, the letter usually contains the key reason for the decision, the date of the decision, and information about what steps can be taken next.

Common reasons denials happen

Denials can happen for many different reasons. Common examples include a finding that a service was not considered medically necessary, a claim that the requested item or service is not covered under the relevant program, missing documentation, an incomplete prior authorization request, a financial or technical eligibility issue, or a decision that the child or adult did not meet the required level of care.

In some situations, the real issue is not whether the need is serious. The issue may simply be that the request did not include the exact forms, records, or language the program expected to see.

Why the reason should be requested in writing

When a service or request is denied, it is important to have the reason in writing. Written explanations help families understand what standard was applied, what information may be missing, and what kind of response may be most helpful on appeal.

If the explanation is unclear, families may want to ask for a full written notice, the specific denial reason, the policy or rule that was used, and the deadline for requesting review or appeal.

Why deadlines matter

Deadlines are one of the most important parts of any denial or appeal process. Many programs allow only a short period of time to challenge a decision. Missing that deadline can make it much harder to preserve rights, continue services, or request a formal review.

Because timelines vary, families should verify the exact deadline directly with the state Medicaid agency, insurer, managed care plan, school system, or program involved in the denial.

What an appeal is

An appeal is a request for the decision to be reviewed again. Depending on the system involved, this may include an internal review, a managed care appeal, an administrative review, or another formal reconsideration process.

An appeal is often the stage where families submit additional records, explain why the service is needed, correct missing information, or show how the original decision may not reflect the child or adult’s actual condition and support needs.

What a fair hearing is

A fair hearing is generally a more formal review process used in many public benefits systems, including Medicaid. It is usually different from an informal phone call or a routine reconsideration request. In a fair hearing, a hearing officer or other designated decision-maker reviews the dispute and the supporting information.

The exact hearing process can vary, and available public materials do not always describe it the same way across all programs. Families should verify current fair hearing procedures directly with the relevant agency or plan.

Records families may want to keep

It can be helpful to keep copies of denial letters, approval letters, prior authorization submissions, prescriptions, evaluations, letters of medical necessity, therapy reports, hospital discharge summaries, waiver notices, emails, call notes, and names of staff members involved in the case.

A simple record of dates can also be very important. Families may want to track when the request was submitted, when the denial was issued, when they called for clarification, and when any appeal paperwork was filed.

Documents that often help

The most helpful documents vary by situation, but often include updated clinical notes, specialist letters, therapy evaluations, hospital records, functional assessments, prescriptions, letters of medical necessity, and documentation showing what will happen if the service or equipment is not approved.

In many cases, a stronger appeal is not about writing more. It is about submitting the clearest records that connect the person’s diagnosis, functional limitations, safety needs, and daily care requirements to the specific service or item being requested.

Important reminder

Appeal rights, hearing rights, continuation-of-benefits rules, and filing deadlines vary by state, program, managed care plan, and insurer. Families should verify the current process directly with the entity that issued the denial.

This page is meant to provide general information and organization support. It should not be treated as legal advice or as a substitute for state-specific guidance, advocacy, or legal representation where needed.

Parent and family caregiver pay

This page provides a national overview of a question many families ask: can a parent or family member be paid to provide care? The answer depends heavily on the state, the program, the person’s age, the type of services approved, and whether the program allows some form of self-direction or family-paid care. This page is intended as a general guide, not a state-by-state legal or policy determination.

Why rules vary so much

Paid parent and family caregiving rules vary widely because Medicaid is not one single program with one single rulebook for every service. Different services may be authorized through waivers, state plan benefits, managed care arrangements, or self-directed models, and those structures can operate differently across states.

As a result, one family may qualify for Medicaid and still not be allowed to have a parent paid under a particular service, while another family in a different state or different program may have that option.

Medicaid eligibility is not the same as caregiver pay

Qualifying for Medicaid does not automatically mean a parent or family member can be paid to provide care. Medicaid eligibility answers one question: whether the child or adult qualifies for coverage or services. Paid caregiver rules answer a different question: who is allowed to provide an approved service and be reimbursed for it.

Those two issues are related, but they are not the same. A family may secure Medicaid coverage and still need separate approval, a different service model, or a different program in order for family-paid caregiving to be possible.

Waiver services, state plan services, and self-direction

Many paid caregiver questions come down to how the service is authorized. Some supports are offered through Medicaid waivers, some are provided through the regular Medicaid state plan, and some are delivered through self-direction or consumer-directed service models.

In some programs, a parent or family member may be excluded as a paid provider. In others, payment may be allowed only under limited circumstances. In self-directed models, the individual or family may sometimes have more choice over who provides care, but the rules still depend on the state and the service.

Why some states allow it and others do not

Some states allow family-paid care in certain programs because the service design, staffing rules, or self-direction authority permits it. Other states restrict payment to non-legally responsible caregivers or only allow payment to certain relatives in limited settings. Program design matters just as much as diagnosis or need.

Even within the same state, one service may allow family-paid care while another does not. That is why it is important to identify the exact program name, not just the general idea of receiving home care or waiver services.

Why age, diagnosis, and level of care may matter

Age can matter because some child-serving programs have different provider rules than adult programs. Diagnosis can matter because some waivers are tied to specific populations, such as developmental disabilities, medically complex children, or behavioral health needs. Level of care can matter because programs designed as alternatives to institutional care may apply different staffing and provider rules than ordinary state-plan services.

Program type also matters. A child may qualify for Medicaid through one route, receive nursing or personal care through another, and have different family-provider rules attached to each.

What families may want to ask

When paid family caregiving is a priority, families may want to ask the state or program: what is the exact name of the service, is it waiver-based or state-plan based, does the program allow self-direction, are parents or legally responsible relatives excluded, and are there different rules for minors and adults?

Those questions often lead to clearer answers than simply asking whether a parent can be paid, because the answer may depend entirely on the program structure behind the service.

Why direct verification matters

Rules can vary by state, program, eligibility category, managed care structure, and the age of the person receiving services. Public materials do not always explain family-paid caregiving rules clearly or consistently, especially when a state uses multiple pathways for children and adults.

For that reason, families should verify current rules directly with the state Medicaid agency, waiver administrator, managed care plan, or service coordinator responsible for the specific program involved.

How to use this page

This page is meant to provide a national framework for understanding the issue. It can help families ask better questions, identify the correct program, and avoid assuming that Medicaid approval automatically answers the caregiver pay question.

For program-specific decisions, families should rely on current state guidance and direct confirmation from the program that would authorize and pay for the service. Families who are trying to identify the right first step may also want to review Who to Call First.

Important reminder

This page is informational only. It does not create legal, financial, tax, or benefits advice. Families should verify current caregiver payment rules, employment rules, and program restrictions directly with the state or program before making employment, care, or financial decisions.

Diagnosis-specific resources

This section highlights a focused group of national organizations that many families use when they need diagnosis-specific information, community support, advocacy, and practical next steps. Diagnosis-specific organizations can be helpful even when a family is also using Medicaid, school-based services, or hospital systems, because they often provide condition-specific education, referrals, family support, and policy resources that broader systems do not always offer.

United Cerebral Palsy

United Cerebral Palsy is a national affiliate network serving children and adults with cerebral palsy and other disabilities. Families may find it helpful for advocacy, community-based referrals, and practical service connections through local affiliates, especially when they are trying to understand long-term supports and everyday disability services.

Epilepsy Foundation

The Epilepsy Foundation is a national, family-focused resource for people affected by seizures and epilepsy. It offers education, training, advocacy, and local foundation connections that can be especially helpful for families looking for seizure information, school resources, and community support beyond the medical setting.

National Down Syndrome Society

The National Down Syndrome Society provides policy advocacy, family resources, community information, and condition-specific education for individuals with Down syndrome and their families. It can be a strong starting point for families who want both practical resources and broader policy and community support.

Autism Society

The Autism Society is a long-established national organization that connects autistic people, families, and community members to resources, advocacy, education, and local support networks. Families may find it especially useful when they need practical resource navigation and community support in addition to medical or school-based services.

National Organization for Rare Disorders

The National Organization for Rare Disorders, often called NORD, is one of the most useful national resources for rare disease communities. It offers disease information, advocacy, patient assistance resources, and connections to patient organizations that can help families locate more condition-specific support.

Family Voices

Family Voices is a family-led national organization focused on children and youth with special health care needs and disabilities, including many medically complex children. Families may find it especially helpful for family support, systems navigation, and state-level connections through Family-to-Family Health Information Centers and related family leadership networks.

Legal and advocacy help

Families often need more than information. They may also need advocacy, parent training, systems navigation, or legally based support. This page highlights a focused set of national entry points that can help families locate the right kind of advocacy support depending on the issue.

National Disability Rights Network and Protection and Advocacy Agencies

The National Disability Rights Network is the national membership organization for the Protection and Advocacy systems and Client Assistance Programs. There is a Protection and Advocacy agency in every state and territory, and these agencies can be an important resource when a family needs rights protection, legally based advocacy, or help related to abuse, neglect, discrimination, access, or public services.

Parent Centers

Parent Centers are one of the most practical advocacy resources for families of children with disabilities. The national Parent Center Hub explains that there are Parent Training and Information Centers and Community Parent Resource Centers serving families across the country. These centers can be especially helpful for school issues, evaluations, IEPs, 504 plans, transition planning, and understanding education rights in a practical way.

Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund

DREDF is a national civil rights law and policy organization focused on disability rights. It can be a useful resource when families are trying to understand broader disability civil rights issues, legal frameworks, access questions, and policy developments that affect education, public programs, and community participation.

Family-led advocacy support

Family Voices is also an important advocacy resource because it is family-led and focused on systems of care for children and youth with special health care needs and disabilities. It can be a strong option when families want support that is grounded in lived experience and family-centered systems advocacy.

When families may want to reach out

Legal and advocacy support may be especially helpful when a family is facing repeated denials, school disputes, discrimination concerns, service access problems, unsafe treatment settings, or major confusion about which system is responsible for help. Some advocacy help is national, but much of the most practical support is state-specific, so local or state-based follow-up may still be the most useful next step.

Important reminder

Advocacy resources vary in scope. Some provide training and information, while others provide legally based advocacy or referral support. Families should verify current program details directly with the organization involved and ask what type of help is actually available in their state or situation.

Financial help and grants

Families often need financial help that goes beyond equipment. Travel for specialized care, therapy costs, emergency needs, and rare-disease-related expenses can create major gaps even when a child or adult has insurance or Medicaid. This section highlights a small group of national resources and resource hubs that may be worth reviewing when those gaps arise.

NORD Patient Assistance Programs

NORD offers patient assistance programs for people living with rare diseases. Available assistance can include support related to medication access, insurance costs, diagnostic testing, travel in some circumstances, caregiver respite, and emergency support. Eligibility, availability, and funding can change, so current program details should be verified directly with NORD.

Christopher & Dana Reeve Foundation

The Reeve Foundation is widely used as a practical resource hub for grants, community support ideas, financial-assistance guidance, and information about durable medical equipment and related support needs. Families may find it helpful as a starting point when they need broader practical assistance and do not know which funding route to try first.

Family-to-Family Health Information Centers

Family Voices helps connect families to Family-to-Family Health Information Centers and affiliate organizations around the country. These centers may not always provide direct grant funding, but they can be very helpful in identifying state and nonprofit resources, short-term help, family support options, and practical next steps when a family is financially overwhelmed.

Travel help

Travel help can be especially important when specialty care is far from home. National support may be limited and diagnosis-specific organizations sometimes fill the gap. For rare disease families, NORD’s patient assistance resources are one of the clearest national examples of travel-related assistance. Families should verify current eligibility and availability directly with the organization involved.

Therapy and emergency support

Therapy grants and emergency family support often change from year to year and may be offered through diagnosis-specific organizations, local nonprofits, hospital-associated funds, or condition-specific family networks rather than one permanent national program. Families may find it helpful to start with national resource hubs and then ask for state or diagnosis-specific referrals.

Important reminder

Funding availability, grant cycles, eligibility criteria, and application windows can change quickly. Families should verify current program details directly with the organization involved and should not assume a grant or assistance program will remain open or available over time.

Special Olympics and community participation

Special Olympics can be more than a sports program. For many families, it can also be a practical entry point to recreation, inclusion, community connection, and participation. It may be especially relevant for people with intellectual and developmental disabilities, though local offerings, age ranges, and program structure can vary by chapter and location. Families should verify current opportunities directly with their state or local Special Olympics program.

What Special Olympics is

Special Olympics is widely known for sports, but it also supports broader inclusion, health, and participation through multiple program models. Official Special Olympics materials describe year-round training and competition, inclusive programming, and community-based opportunities that can support confidence, connection, and activity across a wide age range.

Who families may hear about it for

Families most often hear about Special Olympics for people with intellectual and developmental disabilities. In practice, many families use it not only for sports participation, but also for community-building, friendship, routine, physical activity, and access to inclusion-focused programming.

Young Athletes

Young Athletes is an early childhood sport and play program for children with and without intellectual disabilities ages 2 to 7. It is designed to introduce basic sport skills, movement, and participation in a family- and community-friendly way, which can make it especially useful for families looking for an early entry point into structured activity and inclusion.

Healthy Athletes

Healthy Athletes includes free health screenings and health education in Special Olympics settings. Depending on the program and event, this can include wellness-related support, health access, and practical health information that may be helpful to athletes and families.

Unified Sports and inclusion

Unified Sports brings people with and without intellectual disabilities together on the same team. Families may also encounter Unified Sports and related inclusion models through school and community settings, which can make Special Olympics relevant not only for recreation, but also for social inclusion and belonging.

Why this can matter for families

Families may find Special Olympics useful even when their main daily focus is Medicaid, school, therapy, or medical care. Recreation, participation, friendship, inclusion, and community connection are important parts of quality of life, and structured programs can sometimes provide support that formal service systems do not.

Local variation

Offerings can vary by local chapter, age group, school partnership, event structure, and program location. Some families may find a strong local sports and community program, while others may have access only to limited or occasional offerings. It is usually best to check directly with the local or state program to understand what is currently available.

How this fits with the rest of the site

This resource is not a substitute for Medicaid, school support, legal advocacy, or medical care. Instead, it can be one useful part of a broader support picture. For some families, it may serve as a community-based next step that supports participation and connection alongside the more formal systems described elsewhere on the site.

Important reminder

Special Olympics programming can vary across local programs, and available public materials do not always describe local implementation in the same way. Families should verify current opportunities, eligibility details, age-specific offerings, and local programming directly with the Special Olympics program in their area.

Equipment funding and assistive technology resources

This section highlights organizations and programs that may help families find funding, equipment loans, reuse programs, financing options, or replacement support when Medicaid or insurance does not fully cover needed equipment. Availability, eligibility, and funding limits can change, so families should verify current details directly with each organization.

State Assistive Technology Programs

Every state has an Assistive Technology Act program. These programs can help people find device loan libraries, equipment reutilization programs, financing options, and other practical ways to access assistive technology when standard coverage is limited or denied.

The Kids Equipment Network

The Kids Equipment Network focuses on helping children with special needs access adaptive and durable equipment. This can be especially helpful for families trying to bridge a funding gap for pediatric mobility, positioning, or other supportive equipment.

Christopher & Dana Reeve Foundation

The Reeve Foundation offers guidance on equipment funding, grants, and local loan closet options. It can be a helpful starting point for families looking for practical routes to equipment when direct insurance coverage is not available.

United Cerebral Palsy

United Cerebral Palsy and some local affiliates may help connect families to assistive technology funding, equipment support programs, or community-based resources. Availability can vary by location, so local chapter information is especially important.

Chive Charities

Chive Charities is a nonprofit that has supported people with grants for medical needs, mobility-related equipment, and home accessibility needs. Families should review current eligibility and program details directly with the organization.

Autism Speaks Grants and Family Resources

Autism Speaks maintains family resource information that can help connect families to grants, equipment-related support, and diagnosis-specific assistance. This can be especially useful when equipment needs are tied to autism-related communication, sensory, or daily living supports.

Equipment loan closets

Local equipment loan closets can sometimes provide wheelchairs, bath equipment, lifts, positioning devices, or other durable medical equipment at low cost or no cost. These are often run by nonprofits, community groups, hospitals, or faith-based organizations.

Equipment reuse programs

Reuse and exchange programs can help families locate used equipment that is still safe and functional. These programs can be especially valuable when a child outgrows equipment quickly or when insurance approval is delayed.

Alternative financing programs

Some states offer low-interest or alternative financing programs for assistive technology and disability-related equipment. These are often connected to the state Assistive Technology Act program and may be worth reviewing when grants are not available.
